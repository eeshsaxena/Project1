The LLM's parametric memory already knows about Dr. Vikram Sarabhai. The corpus

\emph{``Dr. Vikram Sarabhai is widely regarded as the father of the Indian space

launching station. After his passing in 1971, leadership of ISRO passed to

\end{quote}

\textbf{Query: ``Which ISRO mission first confirmed water on the Moon?''}

The two Chandrayaan missions (2008 and 2023) both appear in the graph. The
system correctly associated water confirmation with Chandrayaan-1 (2008), since
the Chandrayaan-3 document described landing on the south pole, not water
confirmation. This required the retriever to distinguish between two ISRO
missions that both involve the Moon---a nuanced retrieval task.

\subsection{The Space Telescope Query: A Notable Result}

The most striking result from the live testing was the space telescope conflict.
When asked ``Which is NASA's current primary space telescope?'', the system
returned:

\begin{quote}
\emph{``The James Webb Space Telescope is NASA's current primary space
telescope. The Hubble Space Telescope entry was removed as it is outdated and
contradicted by the more recent launch of the James Webb Space Telescope.''}
\end{quote}

This answer is notable for two reasons. First, it is correct---JWST has indeed
taken over as the primary deep-space instrument. Second, it explicitly tells the
user what it discarded and why. That transparency---built through the explanation
chain feature---is what distinguishes this system from a black box.

\section{Comparison Against Requirements}

\subsection{Functional Requirements Fulfillment}

As shown in Table~\ref{tab:fr_results}, all 12 functional requirements were met
across all four test domains. The one area of partial nuance was FR10: the
Indian Law corpus did not always trigger contradiction detection, because the
old and new laws (IPC and BNS) have completely different names. Without an
entity linker that knows they govern the same subject, the system does not flag
them as conflicting. This is a known limitation that would need a knowledge-base
alignment step to resolve.

\subsection{Non-Functional Requirements Fulfillment}

All five non-functional requirements were verified during testing. Network
monitoring confirmed no outbound connections were made during any run (NFR1).
Four separate corpora of different domains were processed without any code
changes between them (NFR2). The pipeline did not crash on malformed LLM JSON
output during any run thanks to the markdown-stripping parser (NFR3). All
numeric parameters such as decay rate and confidence weights are adjustable in
the central \texttt{CFG} dictionary (NFR4). LLM cache hit rates ranged from
24 to 40 percent across the test runs, saving meaningful amounts of inference
time on repeated prompt patterns (NFR5).

\section{Future Enhancements}

Looking back at the four test runs, a few clear gaps stand out that future work
should address. The contradiction detection in the legal domain was weak because
two laws covering the same subject had completely different names (the system
did not link ``Indian Penal Code'' and ``Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita'' as covering
the same domain without a more sophisticated entity linker). Multi-hop reasoning
would also help: asking ``What is the current law governing the evidence that
convicted the person found guilty under the law that replaced the IPC?'' is the
kind of chain that requires following multiple edges in sequence, which the
current retriever cannot do.


The confidence scores across all domains were clustered around 40--42\%, which
reflects the fact that Qwen's parametric knowledge is already fairly confident
on many of these topics, making it hard for the provided context to move the
entropy needle. A lighter, less-informed LLM would likely produce more varied
and informative confidence scores.

\subsection{Known Limitation: Temporal Decay Reference Year}

During development a subtle design issue was identified and subsequently fixed.
The original implementation of the exponential decay function~[N2] always
computed the age of a fact relative to the \emph{current real-world year}:

\[
  w(t) = e^{-\lambda(Y_{\text{current}} - Y_{\text{fact}})}
\]

This is correct for queries such as ``Who is the current Prime Minister?''
where the user wants the most recent fact. However, for year-specific queries
such as ``Who was the Prime Minister in 2010?'', the original formula would
still apply a large decay penalty to 2010 facts (since they are 16 years
old relative to 2026), even though 2010 is precisely the target year.

The fix implemented in the final version detects a target year from the query
text using \texttt{\_extract\_query\_year()} and passes it as the decay
reference:

\begin{equation}
  w(t, Q_{\text{year}}) = e^{-\lambda \max(0,\; Q_{\text{year}} - Y_{\text{fact}})}
\end{equation}

When \(Q_{\text{year}}\) is detected, facts dated exactly at the query year
receive full weight (\(w = 1.0\)), facts older than the target year are
downweighted proportionally, and any facts newer than the target year are
already excluded by the temporal snapshot filter~[N4]. Without this fix, a
query like ``Who was the CEO of Twitter in 2020?'' would have penalised the
2020 facts despite them being the correct answer.

% ── LIMITATION 2 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Limitation: Negation-Blind Triple Extraction}

The LLM-based triple extractor (Module~A) is prompted to return structured
\texttt{(head, relation, tail, year)} tuples from each document.  The extractor
has no explicit mechanism for capturing negation.  A document sentence such as
``\emph{Aspirin should \textbf{not} be administered to children with fever}''
may cause the extractor to produce the triple
\texttt{(Aspirin, SAFE\_FOR, Children)} --- the semantic opposite of what the
text asserts.  Because the extraction prompt asks for factual relationships, the
word ``not'' is frequently dropped when the LLM converts prose into structured
form.

\textbf{Impact:} Negated facts could reinforce rather than contradict a
competing claim, reducing the conflict detection rate for safety-critical domains.

\textbf{Planned fix:} The extraction prompt should be extended with an explicit
\texttt{"negated": true|false} field in the JSON schema, and a post-processing
step should invert the relation (e.g.,\ \texttt{SAFE\_FOR} $\rightarrow$
\texttt{UNSAFE\_FOR}) when the negation flag is set.

% ── LIMITATION 3 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Limitation: Fixed Decay Rate \texorpdfstring{$\lambda$}{lambda} Across All Domains}

The temporal decay parameter $\lambda = 0.08$ was chosen to give a half-life of
$\ln 2 / 0.08 \approx 8.7$ years, which is appropriate for general factual
knowledge.  However, knowledge stales at very different rates depending on the
domain:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Medical guidelines}: A 5-year-old clinical recommendation can
    be dangerously outdated.  An appropriate $\lambda \approx 0.25$ would decay
    a 5-year-old fact to $e^{-1.25} \approx 0.29$.
    \item \textbf{Constitutional/historical law}: A 50-year-old statute may be
    fully valid today.  A lower $\lambda \approx 0.01$ is more appropriate.
    \item \textbf{Scientific constants}: Facts like the speed of light are
    timeless; $\lambda = 0$ is correct.
\end{itemize}

The current implementation applies a single global $\lambda$ to all domains,
which can cause over-decay in stable domains (unfairly penalising old-but-valid
science facts) and under-decay in fast-moving domains (retaining outdated
medical facts with too high a weight).  A future enhancement should auto-select
$\lambda$ based on the schema intent class output by Module~A or allow per-corpus
configuration.

% ── LIMITATION 4 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Limitation: Low-Variance Entropy Estimate from \texorpdfstring{$n=3$}{n=3} Samples}

The semantic entropy calculation~[C2] samples the LLM $n = 3$ times with
temperature $> 0$ and computes the token-diversity entropy of the resulting
answers.  With only three samples, the estimator has high variance: if two of
the three samples happen to give the same answer by chance, entropy reads as
low even if the LLM is genuinely uncertain.

Formally, with $n = 3$ the maximum possible entropy is $\ln 3 \approx 1.099$
nats.  A reliable entropy estimate over a distribution of $k$ distinct answers
requires $n \gg k$ samples.  For $k = 5$ plausible answer classes (as can
occur in multi-entity queries), three samples cannot distinguish between a
distribution that is nearly uniform (high uncertainty) and one that is strongly
peaked (low uncertainty).

Increasing to $n = 10$ would improve estimate quality but would multiply the
LLM call count per query by more than three, pushing average latency from
$\sim$\!21 seconds to $>$\!60 seconds on CPU hardware.  A batched parallel
sampling strategy using asynchronous Ollama invocations would recover the
accuracy improvement at lower wall-clock cost.

% ── LIMITATION 5 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Limitation: Undated Triples Receive Implicitly Maximum Recency}

When the LLM extractor cannot identify a publication year for a fact ---
either because the source document contains no date, or because the LLM
omits the \texttt{year} field --- the triple is stored with
\texttt{year = NULL} in Neo4j.  The \texttt{\_temporal\_decay} function
handles this case with an explicit guard:

\begin{verbatim}
if not year: return 1.0
\end{verbatim}

Returning $w = 1.0$ means undated facts are treated as \emph{maximally fresh}
--- equivalent to a fact published today.  This is the wrong bias for most
corpora: an undated fact is more likely to be from an unspecified historical
period than from the current year.  If a corpus contains both a dated 2023
document and an undated 1985 document, the undated fact competes at full
weight against the dated 2023 fact, defeating the purpose of temporal decay.

\textbf{Planned fix:} Undated facts should receive a conservative default
weight (e.g., $w = 0.5$, corresponding to $\lambda t \approx 8.7$ years of
age) rather than the maximum weight of 1.0.

% ── LIMITATION 6 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Limitation: PPR and Edge Retrieval Capped at 200 Records}

The Personalised Page\-Rank computation in Module~B queries Neo4j for all
nodes and edges using:

\begin{verbatim}
MATCH (n) RETURN n.id LIMIT 200
MATCH (a)-[r]->(b) RETURN ... LIMIT 200
\end{verbatim}

For the six-document corpora used in this project, the graph contains well
under 200 edges, so the cap is never reached.  However, for a production
corpus with hundreds of documents, the graph could contain thousands of edges.
Truncating at 200 edges means the PPR walk operates on an incomplete graph,
potentially missing high-relevance facts that happen to fall outside the
first 200 results returned by Neo4j's unordered traversal.

\textbf{Impact:} Retrieval recall drops as corpus size grows, because
structurally relevant but unlucky-ordered nodes are silently excluded.

\textbf{Planned fix:} Replace the flat \texttt{LIMIT 200} with a
seed-anchored subgraph expansion:\ run a BFS from the seed entities up
to depth~3 and retrieve only edges reachable from those seeds,
eliminating irrelevant disconnected subgraph components and making the
cap practically hard to reach for topic-focused queries.
