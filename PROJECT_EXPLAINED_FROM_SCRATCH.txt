PART 1 — WHAT IS THE PROBLEM WE ARE SOLVING?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Imagine you are a doctor. You have a pile of 500 medical papers on your desk.
A patient asks: "Is aspirin safe for children with fever?"

You go through your papers and find:
  - A 1985 paper: "Aspirin is effective and safe for childhood fevers."
  - A 2023 WHO guideline: "Aspirin MUST NOT be given to children — it causes
    Reye's Syndrome, a life-threatening brain condition."

If you're a smart doctor, you pick the 2023 paper. The older one is outdated.

Now imagine instead of you, an AI assistant reads the same pile of papers.
The standard AI (LangChain RAG) might say:
  "Aspirin is effective for childhood fevers."
Because it found the 1985 paper and didn't notice the contradiction.

This is the problem. The AI doesn't:
  1. Know that one source contradicts the other
  2. Know which source to trust (newer is better for medical facts)
  3. Tell you how confident it is in its answer

TruthfulRAG v5 fixes all three of these problems.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
PART 2 — WHAT IS AN LLM (LARGE LANGUAGE MODEL)?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

An LLM like ChatGPT, Gemini, or Qwen is a program trained on most of the
internet — Wikipedia, books, news, research papers — up to a certain date.

After training it has "memorised" general knowledge. You can ask it anything
and it gives a confident-sounding answer.

BUT it has two fatal problems:

PROBLEM 1 — Training cutoff
  The LLM was trained on data up to, say, October 2023.
  It knows nothing about what happened after that.
  If you ask "Who is the current Prime Minister of India in 2026?" — it might
  say the wrong person because it doesn't know about elections after 2023.

PROBLEM 2 — Hallucination
  LLMs sometimes confidently make up facts that sound real but aren't.
  They fill in gaps in their knowledge with plausible-sounding nonsense.

SOLUTION → RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)
  Instead of relying only on the LLM's memorised knowledge, you GIVE it
  your own up-to-date documents at the time of asking.
  The LLM then reads those documents and answers based on them.

This is like: you hand the doctor the 500 papers instead of asking them
to remember from their university training 20 years ago.


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
PART 3 — WHAT IS RAG? (RETRIEVAL-AUGMENTED GENERATION)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

RAG = "Find relevant documents, then ask LLM to answer based on them."

The standard way (LangChain RAG) works in 5 steps:

STEP 1 — CHUNKING
  Split each document into small pieces called "chunks".
  Each chunk is about 512 words.
  Why? Because LLMs can only read a limited amount of text at once.

  Example:
    Document: "Aspirin was first synthesised in 1897. It is widely used
               for pain and fever. Early studies showed it was safe for
               children. However recent research showed serious risks."
    Chunk 1: "Aspirin was first synthesised in 1897. It is widely used
               for pain and fever."
    Chunk 2: "Early studies showed it was safe for children. However
               recent research showed serious risks."

STEP 2 — EMBEDDING (Converting Text to Numbers)
  A computer cannot compare text directly.
  So we convert each chunk into a list of numbers called a "vector" or "embedding".
  Example: "Aspirin is safe for children" → [0.23, -0.45, 0.11, ..., 0.89]
                                             (384 numbers)

  These numbers capture the MEANING of the sentence.
  Sentences with similar meanings get similar numbers.
  "Aspirin is safe for kids" → [0.22, -0.44, 0.12, ..., 0.88] (very close!)
  "The Eiffel Tower is in Paris" → [0.91, 0.34, -0.56, ..., 0.12] (very different)

  The tool we use for this is called "sentence-transformers" (all-MiniLM-L6-v2).
  It gives 384-dimensional vectors for any text.

STEP 3 — VECTOR DATABASE STORAGE
  All chunk vectors are stored in a database (FAISS or Chroma).
  Think of it as a filing system where similar meanings are filed near each other.

STEP 4 — RETRIEVAL (Finding Relevant Chunks)
  User asks: "Can children take aspirin for fever?"
  This question is also converted to a vector: [0.21, -0.43, 0.13, ...]
  
  We measure "cosine similarity" between the question vector and all chunk vectors.
  Cosine similarity = angle between two vectors (0=unrelated, 1=identical meaning)
  
  The top 3-5 most similar chunks are retrieved.

STEP 5 — GENERATION (LLM Answers)
  The retrieved chunks are sent to the LLM as context.
  Prompt: "Based on these documents [chunk1 + chunk2], answer: Can children
           take aspirin for fever?"
  LLM reads and generates an answer.

WHY THIS FAILS ON CONFLICTED KNOWLEDGE:
  If chunk1 says "safe" (1985) and chunk2 says "dangerous" (2023),
  BOTH get retrieved and both are sent to the LLM.
  The LLM has no way to know which one is newer.
  It might pick the wrong one, average them, or confuse itself.
  Result: wrong answer ~48% of the time on conflicted queries.

PART 4 — WHAT IS A KNOWLEDGE GRAPH?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

A knowledge graph is a structured way of storing facts as connections.

Instead of storing text, we store FACTS as triples:
  (Subject) --[Relationship]--> (Object)

Examples:
  (Aspirin) --[SAFE_FOR]--> (Children)     [from 1985 paper]
  (Aspirin) --[CAUSES]-->   (ReyeSyndrome)  [from 2023 guideline]
  (Aspirin) --[TREATS]-->   (Fever)         [from multiple papers]
  (Narendra Modi) --[IS_PM_OF]--> (India)   [since 2014]
  (Manmohan Singh) --[WAS_PM_OF]--> (India) [2004-2014]

Each fact = one "edge" (arrow) in the graph.
Each entity = one "node" (circle) in the graph.

WHY IS THIS BETTER THAN CHUNKS?
  1. A chunk is text — you can't reason about it structurally.
     A triple is a structured fact — the computer understands Subject-Relation-Object.

  2. You can TRAVERSE the graph: "Start from Aspirin, find everything connected to it."
     You cannot traverse text chunks this way.

  3. You can detect CONTRADICTIONS: if two triples have the same Subject and 
     Relationship but different Objects — that is a CONFLICT.
     (Aspirin)-[SAFE_FOR]->(Children)   vs
     (Aspirin)-[CONTRAINDICATED_FOR]->(Children)
     → Same subject, related relation, different object = CONFLICT DETECTED.

  4. You can attach metadata to each edge: year, source count, confidence.

We store our knowledge graph in Neo4j — a professional graph database.
Think of Neo4j as MySQL but for graphs instead of tables.

PART 5 — WHAT IS PERSONALISED PAGERANK (PPR)?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

PageRank is the algorithm Google invented to rank web pages.
The idea: important pages are linked to by many other important pages.
A page that has many high-quality links pointing at it ranks higher.

Personalised PageRank (PPR) does the same but starts from specific nodes
(the "seeds") instead of the entire graph.

Imagine the knowledge graph as a city with roads between buildings.
PPR is like: "Start at the Aspirin building. Walk randomly.
             At each intersection, with 20% probability, teleport back
             to the Aspirin building. Walk for 20 steps.
             Which buildings did you visit most often?"

The buildings you visited most = most relevant nodes to the query topic.

In our system:
  User asks: "Can children take aspirin for fever?"
  Seeds = {Aspirin, Children, Fever}  ← entities extracted from query
  PPR walks the graph from these seeds for 20 iterations.
  Each node gets a PPR score = how often the random walk passed through it.
  
  Aspirin → high score (seed)
  Fever → high score (seed)
  ReyeSyndrome → medium score (directly connected to Aspirin)
  WHO → low score (indirectly connected)
  EiffelTower → zero score (completely disconnected from medical domain)

WHY NOT JUST USE KEYWORDS?
  If you search for "PM of India" using keywords, you miss documents that say
  "Prime Minister of Hindustan" or "head of Indian government."
  PPR finds structurally related facts even if they use different words.

PART 6 — WHAT IS ENTROPY IN THIS CONTEXT?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

In information theory, entropy measures UNCERTAINTY or RANDOMNESS.

High entropy = very uncertain / random
Low entropy  = very certain / predictable

Example:
  You flip a fair coin. Heads or tails — 50/50.
  H = -0.5×log(0.5) - 0.5×log(0.5) = 1.0 nats  (HIGH entropy = very uncertain)

  You flip a biased coin that always shows heads.
  H = -1.0×log(1.0) = 0.0 nats  (ZERO entropy = completely certain)

HOW WE USE ENTROPY:
  Step 1: Ask the LLM the same question 3 times WITH NO CONTEXT (no documents).
          "Can children take aspirin for fever?"
          Answer 1: "Yes, aspirin helps with fever."
          Answer 2: "No, aspirin is dangerous for children."
          Answer 3: "It depends on the dose."
          
          These are all different → HIGH entropy = LLM is confused = 1.06 nats
          This is called "parametric entropy" (H_param)
          — what the LLM knows FROM MEMORY without help.

  Step 2: Ask the LLM 3 times WITH a specific document as context.
          Context: "2023 WHO guideline: Aspirin is contraindicated in children."
          Answer 1: "No, children should not take aspirin."
          Answer 2: "No, aspirin is contraindicated."
          Answer 3: "No, give ibuprofen instead."
          
          All three agree → LOW entropy = LLM is now certain = 0.0 nats
          This is called "augmented entropy" (H_aug)

  Step 3: Compute delta_H = H_param - H_aug = 1.06 - 0.0 = +1.06
          Large positive delta_H means this document REALLY helped.
          It reduced the LLM's confusion significantly.
          → This document is useful. Keep it.

  Step 4: If delta_H > threshold tau (e.g. 0.35):
          The document passes the filter — it gets included in the final answer.
          If delta_H < tau: the document didn't help — discard it.

WHY THIS IS BRILLIANT:
  LangChain picks documents that are word-similar to the query.
  v4/v5 picks documents that actually REDUCE THE LLM'S UNCERTAINTY.
  These are not the same thing. A document can sound relevant but not help.

PART 7 — WHAT IS TEMPORAL DECAY? (YOUR NOVEL CONTRIBUTION N2)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

"Temporal" means "relating to time."
"Decay" means "getting weaker over time."

The idea: facts become less trustworthy as they get older.

A 2023 medical guideline is more trustworthy for current treatment than a 1985 
paper — because medical knowledge has advanced significantly.

We model this with an exponential decay formula:

  weight = e^(-lambda × age_in_years)

Where:
  e   = 2.718 (mathematical constant, base of natural log)
  lambda = 0.08 (decay rate — how fast trust drops)
  age = current_year - fact_year

Examples (lambda = 0.08):
  2023 fact: age=3,  weight = e^(-0.08×3)  = e^(-0.24) = 0.79 (79% trust)
  2010 fact: age=16, weight = e^(-0.08×16) = e^(-1.28) = 0.28 (28% trust)
  1990 fact: age=36, weight = e^(-0.08×36) = e^(-2.88) = 0.06 (6% trust)
  1985 fact: age=41, weight = e^(-0.08×41) = e^(-3.28) = 0.04 (4% trust)

WHY EXPONENTIAL? Not linear (100% → 50% → 0%)?
  Knowledge doesn't decay at a constant rate.
  Recent knowledge stays highly trustworthy for a while, then drops faster.
  Exponential curves model this naturally.
  Also: e^(-λt) is never exactly zero — even a 100-year-old fact
  has SOME residual weight (but very small).

HALF-LIFE:
  With lambda=0.08, the "half-life" = ln(2)/0.08 = 8.7 years.
  Meaning: after 8.7 years, a fact has 50% of its original trust weight.

YOUR FIX — YEAR-ANCHORED DECAY:
  Original bug: always compared fact year to 2026 (current year).
  Problem: if user asks "Who was PM of India in 2010?", the 2010 facts
           would get heavily decayed (age=16) even though they are CORRECT
           for that query.
  
  Your fix: detect the target year from the query text.
           If query contains "2010", use 2010 as reference instead of 2026.
           A 2010 fact asked about 2010 → age=0 → weight=1.0 (full trust).

PART 8 — WHAT IS CORROBORATION SCORING? (YOUR NOVEL CONTRIBUTION N1)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Corroboration = multiple independent sources saying the same thing.

If ONE website says "you can cure cancer with lemon juice" — suspicious.
If FIVE independent peer-reviewed journals all say "ibuprofen is safe for children"
— more trustworthy.

We count how many documents support each fact.
When we extract triples:
  Doc1: (Aspirin, TREATS, Fever)  → support_count = 1
  Doc2: (Aspirin, TREATS, Fever)  → support_count = 2 (same fact, second source)
  Doc3: (Aspirin, TREATS, Fever)  → support_count = 3

The score bonus for corroboration:
  corroboration_bonus = log(1 + support_count) × weight

Why logarithmic? Because going from 1→2 sources is more meaningful than
going from 10→11 sources. The first few extra sources add a lot of trust;
additional sources add diminishing returns.

  support=1: log(1+1) = log(2) = 0.69
  support=3: log(1+3) = log(4) = 1.39 (2× bonus for 3 sources)
  support=9: log(1+9) = log(10) = 2.30 (3× bonus for 9 sources)

PART 9 — WHAT IS BM25 + SEMANTIC HYBRID RETRIEVAL? (YOUR CONTRIBUTION N3)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

There are two ways to find relevant information:

WAY 1 — KEYWORD MATCHING (BM25):
  BM25 is an improved version of "count how many query words appear in the doc."
  
  Query: "PM of India"
  Doc A: "PM of India is Narendra Modi" → contains "PM", "of", "India" → HIGH score
  Doc B: "The Indian Prime Minister leads the government" → contains "Indian" → LOW score
  Doc C: "The head of the Indian government is elected" → LOW score
  
  BM25 would rank Doc A first. Docs B and C lose even though they're relevant.
  This is BM25's weakness: misses synonyms and different phrasings.

WAY 2 — SEMANTIC MATCHING (Embeddings):
  Convert everything to vectors, find vectors that point in the same direction.
  
  "PM of India" → vector
  "The Indian Prime Minister leads the government" → vector (similar direction!)
  
  Captures meaning even across different words.
  Weakness: sometimes misses exact keyword matches for rare proper nouns.

YOUR SOLUTION — COMBINE BOTH WITH RRF:
  Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) combines the two ranked lists mathematically.
  
  Formula: RRF_score = 1/(60 + rank_BM25) + 1/(60 + rank_semantic)
  
  Where 60 is a smoothing constant (from the original RRF paper, 2009).
  
  Example:
    Document A: BM25 rank=#1, Semantic rank=#3
    RRF_A = 1/(60+1) + 1/(60+3) = 0.0164 + 0.0159 = 0.0323
    
    Document B: BM25 rank=#5, Semantic rank=#1
    RRF_B = 1/(60+5) + 1/(60+1) = 0.0154 + 0.0164 = 0.0317
    
    Document C: BM25 rank=#2, Semantic rank=#2
    RRF_C = 1/(60+2) + 1/(60+2) = 0.0161 + 0.0161 = 0.0323
    
  WINNER: A and C tie. B is close. Any document that ranks well in BOTH
  systems wins. A document that ranks very poorly in both loses.
  
  This is more robust than using either alone.

PART 10 — WHAT IS DYNAMIC SCHEMA INFERENCE? (YOUR CONTRIBUTION N4)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

A "schema" in a knowledge graph is like a grammar — what types of entities
can exist and what types of relationships between them are valid.

Example medical schema:
  Entity types: [DRUG, CONDITION, PATIENT_GROUP, GUIDELINE]
  Relation types: [TREATS, CAUSES, SAFE_FOR, CONTRAINDICATED_FOR, SUPERSEDES]

Example space science schema:
  Entity types: [MISSION, TELESCOPE, AGENCY, ASTRONAUT, PLANET]
  Relation types: [LAUNCHED_BY, DISCOVERS, SUCCESSOR_TO, ORBITS, FUNDED_BY]

THE OLD WAY (v4):
  You had to manually write these schemas before the system could work.
  Different domain? Rewrite the schema. Time-consuming. Error-prone.

YOUR WAY (v5) — AUTO-INFERENCE:
  Feed 3 sample documents to the LLM.
  Ask: "Based on these documents, what entity types and relation types exist?"
  LLM returns a JSON schema automatically.
  
  This means the SAME CODE works for:
  - Medical corpus → detects DRUG, CONDITION, TREATMENT automatically
  - Legal corpus → detects LAW, COURT, OFFENCE automatically
  - Science corpus → detects SCIENTIST, MISSION, DISCOVERY automatically
  - ANY new domain → no code change needed!

This is what makes TruthfulRAG v5 "domain-agnostic."

PART 11 — WHAT IS THE CONFIDENCE SCORE? (YOUR CONTRIBUTION N5)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

After the answer is generated, v5 attaches a confidence percentage.
This is NOT made up. It is mathematically computed from three signals:

SIGNAL 1 — ENTROPY REDUCTION (40% weight):
  How much did the retrieved document reduce the LLM's confusion?
  Big confusion before, no confusion after → high entropy reduction.
  h_sig = how much delta_H exceeded the threshold tau.
  
  If delta_H = 1.06 and tau = 0.35:
  h_sig = min(1.06/0.35, 2.0) / 2.0 = min(3.03, 2.0) / 2.0 = 1.0
  (capped at 1.0 to prevent one signal dominating)

SIGNAL 2 — CORROBORATION (30% weight):
  How many independent documents support this answer?
  sup_s = min( log(1 + support) / log(6) , 1.0 )
  
  support=1: sup_s = log(2)/log(6) = 0.39  (39%)
  support=5: sup_s = log(6)/log(6) = 1.0   (100%)
  support=9: sup_s = 1.0 (capped at 100%)

SIGNAL 3 — RECENCY (30% weight):
  How recent is the supporting evidence?
  rec_s = exp(-0.05 × year_gap)
  
  year_gap = how many years old is the most recent supporting document
  2023 fact (age 3): rec_s = exp(-0.05×3) = 0.86  (86%)
  2010 fact (age 16): rec_s = exp(-0.05×16) = 0.45 (45%)

FINAL FORMULA:
  confidence = 0.40 × h_sig + 0.30 × sup_s + 0.30 × rec_s
  confidence is always between 0.0 (0%) and 1.0 (100%)

WHY IS 42% "GOOD"?
  When the system says 42%, it means:
  - The LLM already knew the answer from its training (low entropy reduction)
  - There's only 1 supporting document (low corroboration)
  - But the document is recent (high recency)
  
  42% does NOT mean "58% chance the answer is wrong."
  It means "the corpus didn't teach the LLM much it didn't already know."
  
  If you show the same document to a medical student vs a layman:
  - Medical student: "I already knew this." → low confidence gain (like 42%)
  - Layman: "Wow, I had no idea!" → high confidence gain (like 80%)
  
  Our LLM (Qwen 7B) is like the medical student — it already knows
  general medical facts. The corpus barely surprises it.

PART 12 — THE COMPLETE SYSTEM FLOW (STEP BY STEP)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Below is what happens from the moment you upload documents to the final answer.

PHASE 1 — CORPUS LOADING (happens once per corpus, ~60-90 seconds)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Step 1: User selects "corpus_medical.json" and clicks "Load and Build Graph."

Step 2: Schema Inference
  System reads 3 sample documents and sends them to Ollama (local Qwen LLM).
  Prompt: "Looking at these documents, what entity types and relation types
           are present? Return as JSON."
  Ollama returns:
  {
    "entity_types": ["DRUG", "CONDITION", "SYMPTOM", "GUIDELINE"],
    "relation_types": ["TREATS", "CAUSES", "SAFE_FOR", "CONTRAINDICATED_FOR"]
  }
  This is saved as the "schema" for this corpus.

Step 3: Triple Extraction (for each document)
  For each of the 6 documents, the LLM is called with the schema and asked
  to extract all facts as triples.
  
  Document 1 (1985): "Aspirin has been used since 1897 for fever and pain.
                      It is generally considered safe for children."
  → Triples extracted:
    (Aspirin, TREATS, Fever, year=1985)
    (Aspirin, TREATS, Pain, year=1985)
    (Aspirin, SAFE_FOR, Children, year=1985)
  
  Document 2 (2023): "WHO 2023 guidelines: Aspirin is contraindicated in
                      children under 12 with fever due to Reye's Syndrome."
  → Triples extracted:
    (Aspirin, CONTRAINDICATED_FOR, Children, year=2023)
    (Aspirin, CAUSES, ReyeSyndrome, year=2023)
    (WHO, RECOMMENDS, Ibuprofen, year=2023)

Step 4: Corroboration Counting
  If Doc2 also says "Aspirin treats fever" (same triple as Doc1):
  support_count for (Aspirin, TREATS, Fever) becomes 2.

Step 5: Store in Neo4j
  All triples are stored using "MERGE" (upsert) so duplicates are avoided.
  Each edge stores: year, support_count, relation_type.
  
  The Neo4j graph now looks like:
  
  [Aspirin] --TREATS(year=1985, support=2)-----------> [Fever]
  [Aspirin] --SAFE_FOR(year=1985, support=1)----------> [Children]
  [Aspirin] --CONTRAINDICATED_FOR(year=2023, support=1)> [Children]
  [Aspirin] --CAUSES(year=2023, support=1)------------> [ReyeSyndrome]
  [WHO]     --RECOMMENDS(year=2023, support=1)---------> [Ibuprofen]


PHASE 2 — QUERY ANSWERING (happens per question, ~15-25 seconds)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

User types: "Can children take aspirin for fever?"

Step 1: Intent Detection
  LLM classifies the query:
  Intent = "factual_lookup" or "temporal" or "comparison" or "causal"
  For safety queries → intent = "factual_lookup"
  Intent affects the entropy threshold tau used later.
  Higher tau = more strict = only very helpful documents pass.

Step 2: Entity Extraction
  LLM extracts key entities from the query:
  E = ["Children", "Aspirin", "Fever"]
  These become the "seeds" for PageRank.

Step 3: Year Detection (your addition)
  Regex search for any year in the query: r'\b(19|20)\d{2}\b'
  "Can children take aspirin for fever?" → no year found
  snapshot_year = None → use all facts regardless of year
  
  If query was "Was aspirin safe in 1985?" → snapshot_year = 1985
  → Only facts with year ≤ 1985 are included in the graph search.

Step 4: Hybrid Retrieval (your addition)
  BM25 ranking: find graph paths that match query keywords
  Semantic ranking: find graph paths whose meaning is close to query
  Combine with RRF formula
  → Both lists merged into one ranked list of candidates

Step 5: Personalised PageRank
  Seeds = {Aspirin, Children, Fever} (extracted in step 2)
  Run 20 iterations of PPR on the Neo4j graph.
  Each node gets a PPR score reflecting structural relevance.
  
  Results:
  Aspirin: 0.45 (seed — very high)
  Fever: 0.38 (seed — high)
  Children: 0.35 (seed — high)
  ReyeSyndrome: 0.22 (directly connected to Aspirin)
  Ibuprofen: 0.15 (connected via WHO→RECOMMENDS)

Step 6: Combined Scoring (your addition)
  For each edge (knowledge path) in the graph:
  
  FORMULA:
  score = ref_p × hub_penalty × (1 + ppr_avg) × decay × (1 + log(support)×w)
  
  Where:
    ref_p       = how much this edge's entities match the query entities
    hub_penalty = reduce score if node has >10 connections (too generic)
    ppr_avg     = average PPR score of source and target nodes
    decay       = e^(-0.08 × (2026 - fact_year))
    corroborate = log(1 + support_count) × weight
  
  Computing for our edges:
  
  Edge A: (Aspirin)-[SAFE_FOR]->(Children) year=1985, support=1
    ref_p=0.85, hub_pen=1.0, ppr=(0.45+0.35)/2=0.40
    decay = e^(-0.08×41) = 0.036
    corroborate = log(2)×0.8 = 0.55
    score = 0.85 × 1.0 × (1+0.40) × 0.036 × (1+0.55) = 0.067
  
  Edge B: (Aspirin)-[CONTRAINDICATED_FOR]->(Children) year=2023, support=1
    ref_p=0.88, hub_pen=1.0, ppr=(0.45+0.35)/2=0.40
    decay = e^(-0.08×3) = 0.787
    corroborate = log(2)×0.8 = 0.55
    score = 0.88 × 1.0 × (1+0.40) × 0.787 × (1+0.55) = 1.508
  
  Edge C: (Aspirin)-[CAUSES]->(ReyeSyndrome) year=2023, support=1
    ref_p=0.62, ppr=(0.45+0.22)/2=0.34, decay=0.787
    score = 0.62 × 1.0 × 1.34 × 0.787 × 1.55 = 1.013
  
  RANKING: B(1.508) > C(1.013) >> A(0.067)
  Take top K = 3: [Edge B, Edge C, Edge A]

Step 7: Contradiction Detection
  Check: do any two paths have same (head, relation_domain) but different tails?
  Edge A: (Aspirin, SAFE_FOR, Children) ← says aspirin IS safe
  Edge B: (Aspirin, CONTRAINDICATED_FOR, Children) ← says aspirin NOT safe
  Same subject (Aspirin), same domain (safety+children), different conclusion
  → CONFLICT FLAGGED!
  
  Resolution: Edge A has score 0.067, Edge B has score 1.508.
  Edge B wins. Edge A is marked as "removed — superseded."
  Reason stored: "Lower temporal decay weight (year 1985 vs 2023)."

Step 8: Entropy Calculation
  Surviving paths: [Edge B, Edge C]
  
  First: H_param (LLM uncertainty without any context):
  Ask LLM "Can children take aspirin for fever?" 3 times.
  Ans1: "Yes, it's effective"
  Ans2: "No, dangerous"
  Ans3: "Maybe in small doses"
  → 3 different answers → H_param = 1.06 nats (very uncertain)
  
  Then for Edge B (2023 contraindication guideline):
  Ask LLM 3 times WITH Edge B as context.
  Ans1: "No, contraindicated"
  Ans2: "No, Reye's Syndrome risk"
  Ans3: "No, use ibuprofen instead"
  → All agree → H_aug_B = 0.0 nats
  → delta_H_B = 1.06 - 0.0 = +1.06 >> tau(0.35) ✓ KEEP
  
  For Edge C (Aspirin causes Reye's Syndrome):
  Ask LLM 3 times WITH Edge C as context.
  Ans1: "Avoid it — causes Reye's Syndrome"
  Ans2: "No, dangerous"
  Ans3: "No, it causes brain damage in children"
  → H_aug_C ≈ 0.1 nats
  → delta_H_C = 1.06 - 0.1 = +0.96 >> tau(0.35) ✓ KEEP

Step 9: Confidence Score
  Best path: Edge B (delta_H = 1.06, support = 1, year = 2023)
  
  h_sig = min(1.06 / 0.35, 2.0) / 2.0 = 1.0
  sup_s = log(1+1) / log(6) = 0.39
  rec_s = exp(-0.05 × 3) = 0.86
  
  confidence = 0.40×1.0 + 0.30×0.39 + 0.30×0.86
             = 0.40 + 0.117 + 0.258
             = 0.775 → 78%

Step 10: Answer Generation
  Context sent to LLM: [Edge B + Edge C text]
  "Aspirin --CONTRAINDICATED_FOR--> Children [2023 WHO guideline]"
  "Aspirin --CAUSES--> ReyeSyndrome [2023 guideline]"
  
  LLM generates:
  "Children should NOT take aspirin for fever.
   Aspirin is contraindicated in children under 12 because it can cause
   Reye's Syndrome — a rare but life-threatening condition affecting the
   brain and liver. Use ibuprofen or paracetamol instead."
  
  Confidence: 78%
  
  Explanation chain shown to user:
  ✓ KEPT: Aspirin --CONTRAINDICATED_FOR--> Children [2023] (decay: 0.79)
  ✓ KEPT: Aspirin --CAUSES--> ReyeSyndrome [2023] (decay: 0.79)
  ✗ REMOVED: Aspirin --SAFE_FOR--> Children [1985] (decay: 0.036, stale)

PART 13 — WHAT IS OLLAMA?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Ollama is software that lets you run LLMs locally on your own laptop.

Without Ollama: you must call OpenAI's servers, pay per API call, send your
data to the cloud, and be dependent on internet connectivity.

With Ollama: the LLM (Qwen2.5-7B) runs on your own CPU/GPU.
No internet needed. No API cost. Your data never leaves your machine.

Ollama runs as a service on port 11434.
Our code talks to it like:
  POST http://localhost:11434/api/generate
  Body: {"model": "qwen2.5:7b", "prompt": "your prompt here"}

The "7B" means 7 Billion parameters — the model's internal weights.
This is small enough to run on 16GB RAM but large enough to be intelligent.

PART 14 — WHAT IS NEO4J?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Neo4j is a graph database — the most popular one in the world.

Normal databases (MySQL, SQLite) store data in rows and columns (tables).
Neo4j stores data as nodes (circles) and edges (arrows between circles).

We talk to Neo4j using a language called "Cypher":

  // Create a node
  CREATE (a:Entity {id: "Aspirin"})

  // Create a relationship
  MATCH (a:Entity {id:"Aspirin"}), (b:Entity {id:"Children"})
  MERGE (a)-[r:SAFE_FOR {year:1985, support:1}]->(b)

  // Find everything connected to Aspirin
  MATCH (n {id:"Aspirin"})-[r]->(m)
  RETURN type(r), m.id, r.year, r.support

Neo4j runs as a service on port 7687.
Our code connects using the langchain_neo4j library.

PART 16 — WHAT IS THE LRU CACHE?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LRU = Least Recently Used.

The problem: for each query, v5 calls the LLM many many times:
  - Schema inference: 1 call
  - Triple extraction: 1 call per document (6 documents = 6 calls)
  - Intent detection: 1 call
  - Entity extraction: 1 call
  - Entropy without context: 3 calls
  - Entropy with context: 3 calls per surviving path
  - Final answer: 1 call
  TOTAL: ~15-20 LLM calls per query on CPU

Each LLM call takes 3-8 seconds. This adds up to 45-160 seconds.

The Cache Solution:
  We compute an MD5 hash of each prompt.
  If we've seen the exact same prompt before → return the cached result instantly.
  No LLM call needed. 0 seconds instead of 3-8 seconds.

  This works because:
  - Schema inference uses the same sample docs → always the same prompt → cache hit
  - Entity extraction for "same" query → cache hit
  - Entropy sampling without context → same question → cache hit

  In testing: 35-40% of all LLM calls were served from cache.
  This saves 35% of total inference time.

PART 17 — ALL 6 KNOWN LIMITATIONS (DOCUMENTED HONESTLY)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

L1 — YEAR-ANCHORED DECAY (FIXED IN v5):
  Bug: decay always computed relative to 2026, not query year.
  Fix: extract target year from query, use it as reference.
  Status: RESOLVED.

L2 — NEGATION-BLIND EXTRACTOR:
  Problem: "Aspirin is NOT safe for children" might become
           (Aspirin, SAFE_FOR, Children) — wrong direction!
  The word "not" is silently dropped by many LLMs during extraction.
  Fix needed: add "negated: true/false" field to extraction schema.
  Status: OPEN.

L3 — FIXED LAMBDA FOR ALL DOMAINS:
  lambda = 0.08 gives 8.7-year half-life.
  Medical facts go stale in 5 years → lambda too low.
  Historical law stays valid for 50+ years → lambda too high.
  Fix: auto-select lambda based on detected domain type.
  Status: OPEN.

L4 — ONLY 3 ENTROPY SAMPLES:
  3 samples → maximum entropy = ln(3) = 1.1 nats.
  With more than 3 distinct possible answers, estimator is noisy.
  Proper statistical estimate needs n=10+ samples.
  But n=10 triples the LLM call count → much slower.
  Fix: async batched sampling to get n=10 without proportional time increase.
  Status: OPEN.

L5 — UNDATED FACTS GET FULL WEIGHT:
  If document has no year → fact stored with year=NULL.
  Current code: if not year: return 1.0  (full weight!)
  A 1960 undated fact beats a 2020 dated fact. Wrong.
  Fix: default undated weight = 0.5 (conservative).
  Status: OPEN.

L6 — PPR GRAPH CAPPED AT 200 NODES/EDGES:
  Code: MATCH (n) RETURN n.id LIMIT 200
  For 6-document corpora: fine (<60 edges typically).
  For 1000-document corpora: miss ~95% of facts.
  Fix: BFS from seed entities, only retrieve connected subgraph.
  Status: OPEN (irrelevant for current corpus size).

PART 20 — IN ONE PARAGRAPH
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

TruthfulRAG v5 is a system that answers questions from your own documents
honestly and accurately — even when those documents contradict each other.
Instead of treating documents as flat text chunks (like ChatGPT plugins do),
it extracts structured facts from them and stores them in a relationship graph.
When a question arrives, it uses a graph-walking algorithm (PPR) to find
relevant facts, applies a mathematical decay formula to downweight old facts,
counts how many sources agree on each fact, detects when two facts contradict
each other and automatically picks the newer more-trusted one, measures how
much each fact helps the AI (using entropy), generates the answer only from
the selected facts, and returns a confidence score that reflects how much
the documents actually helped — all running locally with no internet or cloud
required. The result is 93% accuracy on queries where the source documents
contained contradictory information — compared to 52% for the standard approach.
